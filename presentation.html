Motivation

  • Crystallization is critical in pharmaceutical & chemical manufacturing
  • Narrow particle size distribution (PSD) ensures product quality
  • Continuous crystallization offers better consistency than batch methods
  • Particle suspension quality directly impacts agglomeration, breakage, and blockage
Key challenge: One-way coupled models (LPT) fail to capture fluid–particle feedback at higher solid loadings. We need fully resolved simulations.

The Archimedes Tube Crystallizer

  • Small-scale continuous crystallizer
  • Coiled tube design with air-segmented flow
  • Taylor & Dean vortices drive particle suspension
  • Narrow residence time distribution (RTD) via plug flow

Key parameters:
Coil diameter \(d_{ct} = 50\) mm, tube diameter \(d_i = 5\) mm,
pitch \(P = 7\) mm, 45 coils

Mathematical Model: Governing Equations

Mixed fluid-particle domain: \(\Omega_T = \Omega_f \cup \bigcup_{i=1}^{N} \Omega_i\)

Incompressible Navier–Stokes on \(\Omega_f\)

\[\rho_f \left(\frac{\partial \mathbf{u}}{\partial t} + \mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla\mathbf{u}\right) - \nabla\cdot\boldsymbol{\sigma} = \mathbf{0}\] \[\nabla\cdot\mathbf{u} = 0\]

Cauchy stress tensor

For a Newtonian fluid:

\[\boldsymbol{\sigma} = -pI + \mu_f\left[\nabla\mathbf{u} + (\nabla\mathbf{u})^\top\right]\]

Rigid-Body Constraint & Two-Way Coupling

Inside each particle domain \(\Omega_i\), the velocity field satisfies:

\[\mathbf{u}(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{U}_i + \boldsymbol{\omega}_i \times (\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{X}_i)\]
  • \(\mathbf{U}_i\): translational velocity of particle \(i\)
  • \(\boldsymbol{\omega}_i\): angular velocity of particle \(i\)
  • \(\mathbf{X}_i\): center of mass of particle \(i\)
This constraint couples the fluid to the particle motion and enables full two-way momentum exchange — the key difference to one-way coupled LPT approaches.

Fictitious Boundary Method (FBM)

Key Idea

  • Governing equations extended to full domain \(\Omega_T\)
  • Rigid-body constraints enforced in particle regions
  • Single-domain formulation on a fixed mesh
  • No re-meshing needed as particles move

Force & Torque Computation

Hydrodynamic force and torque via volume integrals:

\[\mathbf{F}_i = -\int_{\Omega_T} \boldsymbol{\sigma}\cdot\nabla\alpha_i\,d\mathbf{x}\]
\[\mathbf{T}_i = -\int_{\Omega_T} (\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{X}_i) \times (\boldsymbol{\sigma}\cdot\nabla\alpha_i)\,d\mathbf{x}\]

where \(\alpha_i\) is the indicator function of particle \(i\) and \(\mathbf{X}_i\) its center. This avoids explicit surface integration and is well-suited for structured meshes.

Particle Dynamics & Discretization

Newton–Euler Equations

\[M_i \frac{d\mathbf{U}_i}{dt} = \Delta M_i \mathbf{g} + \mathbf{F}_i + \mathbf{F}_i^{\text{col}}\]
\[\mathbf{I}_i \frac{d\boldsymbol{\omega}_i}{dt} + \boldsymbol{\omega}_i \times (\mathbf{I}_i \boldsymbol{\omega}_i) = \mathbf{T}_i\]

Two-way coupling: boundary condition in particle domain feeds back into the flow.

Discretization

  • Time: strongly A-stable fractional-step-θ scheme
  • Space: hexahedral meshes, \(Q_2/P_1^{\text{disc}}\) elements
  • Rigid-body constraints enforced strongly on all nodes within particles
  • Parallel domain decomposition

Hard Contact Model

  • Nonsmooth hard-contact formulation with Coulomb friction
  • No artificial spring–dashpot energies (unlike soft-sphere DEM)
  • Contacts formulated as point-wise constraints via the Delassus operator
  • Solved iteratively with Projected Gauss–Seidel (PGS)
\[\mathbf{p}_i^{k+1} = \Pi_{\mathcal{K}_i}\!\left(\mathbf{p}_i^k - \omega \mathbf{W}_{ii}^{-1}\hat{\mathbf{g}}_i\right)\]

Baumgarte stabilization for numerical stability • Parallelized via ghost-body velocity exchange across subdomains • Implementation based on a fork of the Physics Engine (pe) framework

Why DNS over LPT?

FeatureLPT / S–NDNS (FEM-FBM)
CouplingOne-way (drag only)Full two-way
ForcesDrag correlationResolved from stress tensor
Lift & torqueRequires extra closuresNaturally captured
Local \(\phi\) dependenceNot includedInherently resolved
Particle–particle interactionNeglectedCollisions + lubrication
Wake effectsFrozen carrier flowVortex shedding captured
Dense suspensionsInaccurateValid even at high \(\phi\)

S–N drag has no \(\phi\)-dependence and under-predicts drag in dense/stacking configurations.

From One-Way LPT to Four-Way-Coupled Euler–Lagrange

Physics contributionOne-way LPTFour-way Euler–Lagrange
Fluid → particleDrag from prescribed flow fieldDrag/lift/added effects from solved carrier flow
Particle → fluidNeglectedMomentum source term from all particles
Particle–particle couplingUsually neglectedCollisions + frictional contact model
Hydrodynamic interactionsVia empirical correctionsSub-grid closures (e.g., hindered drag/lubrication)

Four-way coupling = two-way fluid–particle exchange + particle collisions/contact. It retains tractable cost while addressing dense-suspension limitations of one-way LPT.

What Is Needed for Reliable Four-Way-Coupled Euler–Lagrange?

Model Ingredients

  • Transient carrier-phase solver (RANS/LES) with particle momentum source coupling
  • Consistent force closures: drag, lift, pressure-gradient, added-mass (as needed)
  • Collision/contact treatment (hard-sphere or soft-sphere DEM) with friction/restitution
  • Near-contact hydrodynamics / hindered-settling corrections for high local \(\phi\)

Numerics & Calibration

  • Interpolation and mapping operators that conserve momentum between phases
  • Time-step constraints from collision time scales + CFL of carrier flow
  • Validation data or DNS-informed closures for dense regimes and wall effects
  • Scalable parallel implementation for \(10^6\!+\) particles and long residence times

DNS and Four-Way Euler–Lagrange: Comparison and Complementarity

AspectDNS (FEM-FBM)4-way Euler–LagrangeBest use in workflow
FidelityInterface-resolved, minimal closuresClosure-based, unresolved particle-scale flowUse DNS as reference truth and closure source
Computational costVery highModerate to highUse 4-way E–L for design-space exploration
Particle count / horizonLimited \(N_p\), short-to-medium timesLarge \(N_p\), industrial time scalesUse 4-way E–L for scale-up and optimization
Dense suspension behaviorDirectly resolvedDepends on closures/contact modelContinuously calibrate E–L with targeted DNS cases

Practical strategy: DNS explains mechanisms and builds trustworthy closures; four-way Euler–Lagrange transfers that physics to reactor-scale, computationally affordable simulations.

Three Quantitative Metrics

Introduced to classify suspension regimes beyond qualitative visual inspection:

1. Axial Distribution \(\overline\phi(s)\)

Mean solid volume fraction along the slug centerline. Captures rear-loading vs. uniform distribution.

2. Radial Index \(I_r\)

Uniformity of cross-sectional distribution. \(I_r \to 1\): well-mixed; \(I_r \ll 1\): wall accumulation.

3. Vertical Asymmetry \(A_y\)

Particle imbalance across the mid-plane. \(A_y \approx 0\): symmetric; \(A_y \to -1\): gravity-dominated.

Linking Metrics to Flow Regimes

Flow Regime\(\overline\phi(s)\)\(I_r\)\(A_y\)
Green (fully mixed) Nearly flat \(\to 1\) \(\approx 0\)
Yellow Moderate spread \(0.5 < I_r < 0.8\) \(\ll 0\)
Red/Yellow Rear-biased \(\sim 0.4\text{–}0.5\) \(\approx -0.95\)
Red (gravitational) Peak at rear \(\lesssim 0.5\) \(\to -1\)
Combined interpretation of all three metrics is essential — any single metric alone can be misleading (e.g., moderate \(I_r\) in red regime is an artifact of rear stacking geometry).

Regime Transitions: The Full Picture

Green → Yellow → Red/Yellow → Red
  • Green → Yellow: Upper-vortex activity collapses, particles settle to lower half (\(A_y\) plunges toward −1), \(I_r\) drops from 0.90 to 0.57–0.77
  • Yellow → Red/Yellow: Rear-bias intensifies, middle radial bins deplete, stacking & poor recirculation
  • Red/Yellow → Red: Suspension collapses into dense bottom-rear cluster, \(A_y = -1.0\), front empty

Meaningful upper-half particle population and front-filling occur only in the green regime.

Conclusion

  • Fully resolved DNS is feasible for predicting particle suspension in the ATC
  • 8 simulations across 4 flow regimes match the experimental flow map with high fidelity
  • Three quantitative metrics (\(\overline\phi(s)\), \(I_r\), \(A_y\)) enable automated, objective regime classification
  • DNS captures effects inaccessible to LPT: momentum exchange, stacking, wall-ward clustering, lubrication
  • Combined metric interpretation avoids misleading single-measure conclusions
The DNS framework provides a mechanistic foundation for high-fidelity crystallizer design.

Outlook & Future Work

  • Extend DNS to larger particle numbers using parallel multigrid FEM solvers
  • Integrate with population balance equations (PBEs) for crystal growth modeling
  • Explore how the proposed metrics inform optimization and scale-up of the ATC
  • Develop DNS-informed closure models for cheaper simulation approaches
